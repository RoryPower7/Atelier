Verbs & tenses

11 forms

The single most common Q3 question type. You'll be asked to find one verb in a specific tense or form. Memorise the ending pattern for each — that's how you spot them at a glance. One example is enough for full marks; extra material loses marks.

Présent de l'indicatif

Standard
Present tense
The default tense for habits, general truths, and ongoing actions. Rarely asked for directly — but watch for it as a contrast in manipulation questions.
-er verbs-e -es -e -ons -ez -ent
-ir verbs-is -is -it -issons -issez -issent
-re verbs-s -s -∅ -ons -ez -ent
Elle habite à Paris depuis cinq ans.
She has lived in Paris for five years.

Passé composé

Very common
Perfect tense (past)
Two words: auxiliary verb (avoir or être) in the present tense + past participle. Used for completed actions in the past.
Look for a / as / ai / avons / avez / ont or est / sont / suis / es / sommes / êtes, immediately followed by a past participle (-é, -i, -u, -is, -it).
Billie a découvert le film Swagger.
Billie discovered the film Swagger.
Always quote both words (a découvert). Quoting just the past participle (découvert) loses marks — it's the participle alone, not the tense.

Imparfait

Very common
Imperfect tense
Used for ongoing or repeated past actions ("was doing", "used to do"). Always one word.
All verbs-ais -ais -ait -ions -iez -aient
Look at the verb's ending. If it ends in -ais, -ait, -ions, -iez, -aient AND there's no auxiliary, it's imparfait.
Quand j'étais petit, je jouais au foot.
When I was small, I used to play football.
-ions / -iez endings also appear in présent (nous/vous) and conditionnel. Check the stem: imparfait keeps the same stem as the nous present form.

Plus-que-parfait

Periodic
Pluperfect tense
"Had done" — an action completed before another past action. Avoir/être in the imparfait + past participle.
Look for avait / avaient / avais / avions / aviez or était / étaient / étais / étions / étiez followed by a past participle.
Il avait fini ses devoirs avant le dîner.
He had finished his homework before dinner.

Futur simple

Common
Simple future tense
One word. Built from the infinitive + ending. Used for predictions and definite future actions.
All verbs-ai -as -a -ons -ez -ont
Find a verb where the infinitive is still visible (you can see the -r-) followed by the future ending: parlera, finirai, prendront.
Demain, nous partirons à six heures.
Tomorrow we will leave at six.
Don't confuse with conditionnel. Futur stem + -ai/-as/-a/-ons/-ez/-ont. Conditionnel = same stem + -ais/-ait/-ions/-iez/-aient.

Futur proche

Periodic
Near future
aller (present) + infinitive. Translates as "going to do".
Look for vais / vas / va / allons / allez / vont immediately followed by a verb in the infinitive.
Je vais voir mes grands-parents ce week-end.
I am going to see my grandparents this weekend.

Conditionnel

Common
Conditional mood
"Would do". Built from the future stem + imparfait endings. Common after si + imparfait clauses.
All verbs-rais -rais -rait -rions -riez -raient
Verb has the infinitive (still ends in -r) followed by -ais, -ait, -ions, -iez, -aient. The -r- right before the ending is the giveaway.
Si j'étais riche, j'achèterais une maison.
If I were rich, I would buy a house.

Subjonctif présent

Periodic
Present subjunctive
Used after expressions of doubt, emotion, necessity, or will — most often after il faut que, je veux que, bien que, pour que, avant que.
All verbs-e -es -e -ions -iez -ent
Find a verb after que in one of these triggers: il faut que, je veux que, bien que, à condition que, avant que, pour que.
Il faut que tu fasses tes devoirs.
You must do your homework.
Subjunctive forms of irregular verbs (être → sois, soit, soyons; avoir → aie, ait, ayons; faire → fasse; aller → aille; pouvoir → puisse) are common in exam texts.

Impératif

Periodic
Imperative (command form)
Used for orders, advice, and instructions. No subject pronoun. Only 3 persons: tu, nous, vous.
A verb at the start of a sentence or clause with no subject, often followed by an exclamation mark or punctuated as advice.
Écoutez attentivement ! Prends ton manteau.
Listen carefully! Take your coat.

Participe présent

Very common
Present participle (-ant)
Ends in -ant. Often preceded by en (= gérondif: "by doing / while doing"). Built from the nous form of the present, replacing -ons with -ant.
Any single word ending in -ant that comes from a verb. Watch out for adjectives that also end in -ant (intéressant) — those are NOT participe présent.
Il a réagi en criant sur les élèves.
He reacted by shouting at the students.
Exclude pure adjectives: intéressant, charmant, fatigant. These describe a noun, not an action. The participle is always a verb form.

Infinitif

Standard
Infinitive
The dictionary form of the verb. Ends in -er, -ir, -re, or -oir. Often follows a preposition (à, de, pour, sans) or a modal verb (pouvoir, devoir, vouloir, savoir).
Look after a preposition (à manger, de partir, pour finir, sans dire) or after a modal verb (je veux aller, il peut faire, on doit étudier).
Elle veut devenir infirmière.
She wants to become a nurse.
P

Pronouns — les pronoms

7 types

Pronouns replace nouns. Q3 will often ask you to find a specific type or to replace a phrase with the appropriate pronoun. Position matters: most object pronouns go before the verb, not after.

Subject pronouns

Standard
Les pronoms sujets
je, tu, il, elle, on, nous, vous, ils, elles. Always come before the verb. The verb conjugates to match.
On ne sait jamais. Elles sont parties hier.
You never know. They (f) left yesterday.

Direct object pronouns

Common
COD — pronoms compléments d'objet direct
Replace a noun directly affected by the verb (no preposition). me, te, le, la, nous, vous, les. Go before the conjugated verb.
Find a verb where the action passes directly to its target — and a small pronoun (le, la, les, me, te) sitting just before that verb.
Je les ai vus au cinéma. Tu le connais ?
I saw them at the cinema. Do you know him?
In the passé composé, the past participle agrees with a direct object that comes before (les livres que j'ai lus, not lu).

Indirect object pronouns

Common
COI — pronoms compléments d'objet indirect
Replace nouns introduced by à (à quelqu'un). Forms: me, te, lui, nous, vous, leur. Lui = to him OR to her; leur = to them.
The verb usually takes à (parler à, dire à, donner à, répondre à, téléphoner à). The pronoun replaces à + person.
Je lui ai parlé hier. On leur donne des cadeaux.
I spoke to him/her yesterday. We give them gifts.

Relative pronouns

Very common
qui · que · dont · où
Link a noun to a relative clause. Pick the right one based on what the noun does in the clause:
quisubject (= who/which does)
quedirect object (= whom/which gets)
dontreplaces de + noun
oùplace or time
Le livre que j'ai lu, l'auteur dont on parle, la ville où j'habite.
The book that I read, the author we are talking about, the city where I live.

The pronoun en

Periodic
en
Replaces de + noun (a thing). Also replaces nouns introduced by du, de la, des. Translates loosely as "some / of it / of them".
A small word en sitting just before the verb, where the verb's structure uses de (parler de, avoir besoin de, venir de).
Tu as des frères ? — Oui, j'en ai deux. Il en parle souvent.
Do you have brothers? — Yes, I have two (of them). He talks about it often.

The pronoun y

Periodic
y
Replaces à + place (where to) or à + thing (about something). Means "there" or "to it / about it".
Single letter y before the verb, where the verb either uses à (penser à, réfléchir à) or refers to a place.
Je vais à Paris ? — Non, j'y suis déjà.
Am I going to Paris? — No, I'm already there.

Demonstrative pronouns

Periodic
celui · celle · ceux · celles
"This one / that one / the one(s)". Replace a noun without naming it again. Agree in gender and number.
m.s.celui
f.s.celle
m.pl.ceux
f.pl.celles
Ce livre n'est pas celui que j'ai acheté.
This book is not the one I bought.
R

Reflexive — verbes pronominaux

Spot guide

A reflexive verb has a pronoun that refers back to the subject. Common ask in Q3: "Find an example of a reflexive verb in the second section." One of the easiest 5 marks on the paper once you know what to look for.

What they look like

Very common
se + verb
In the infinitive, every reflexive verb starts with se (or s' before a vowel): se laver, s'habiller, se réveiller, s'amuser. The se changes to match the subject when conjugated.
jeme lave
tute laves
il / elle / onse lave
nousnous lavons
vousvous lavez
ils / ellesse lavent
Elle se réveille à sept heures. Nous nous habillons rapidement.
She wakes up at seven. We get dressed quickly.

How to spot one in a text

Method
Le test rapide
Scan for me, te, se, nous, vous sitting just before a verb, where that pronoun is the same person as the subject.
If the subject is il and you see se + verb → reflexive. If the subject is je and you see me + verb → reflexive. The pronoun "mirrors" the subject.
Billie s'intéresse au cinéma. Il se sent bien.
Billie is interested in cinema. He feels good.
Don't confuse with object pronouns. Je le vois = le refers to someone else (object). Je me lave = me refers to the same person as je (reflexive).

Common reflexive verbs in past papers

Useful list
À mémoriser
These show up repeatedly in exam texts. If you can recognise these, you'll spot the reflexive in seconds:
se sentirto feel
s'intéresser àto be interested in
se rendre compteto realise
se débrouillerto manage
s'occuper deto look after
s'habituer àto get used to
se passerto happen
se trouverto be located
se demanderto wonder
s'adapterto adapt

Reflexive in the past tense

Watch out
Passé composé pronominal
All reflexive verbs use être (never avoir) in the perfect tense. The past participle usually agrees with the subject.
Elle s'est levée tôt. Ils se sont disputés.
She got up early. They argued.
When asked to quote in past tense, include all three words: pronoun + auxiliary + participle (s'est levée, not just levée).
A

Adjectives — les adjectifs

6 types

Adjectives modify nouns and agree in gender and number. Q3 often asks you to find a specific type or identify what an adjective agrees with. Watch the endings: -e for feminine, -s for plural, -es for both.

Possessive adjectives

Very common
Adjectifs possessifs
Show ownership. Agree with the thing owned, not the owner.
mymon · ma · mes
your (tu)ton · ta · tes
his/her/itsson · sa · ses
ournotre · notre · nos
your (vous)votre · votre · vos
theirleur · leur · leurs
Son père, sa mère et ses frères vivent à Paris.
His/her father, mother and brothers live in Paris.
Use mon, ton, son (not ma/ta/sa) before a feminine noun starting with a vowel: mon amie, son école.

Demonstrative adjectives

Common
Adjectifs démonstratifs
"This / that / these / those". Always followed by a noun.
m.s.ce (cet before vowel)
f.s.cette
m./f. pl.ces
Ce livre, cet appartement, cette idée, ces enfants.
This book, this apartment, this idea, these children.

Indefinite adjectives

Periodic
Adjectifs indéfinis
Refer to unspecified quantity or identity. The most common ones are easy to recognise once you know the list.
chaqueeach / every
plusieursseveral
quelquesa few
certainscertain / some
tous · toute · toutesall
mêmesame
autreother
aucun(e)no / not any
Chaque élève reçoit plusieurs livres.
Each student receives several books.

Interrogative quel

Periodic
Adjectif interrogatif
"Which / what". Agrees with the noun it modifies. Used in questions.
m.s.quel
f.s.quelle
m.pl.quels
f.pl.quelles
Quelle heure est-il ? Quels sont vos projets ?
What time is it? What are your plans?

Comparative

Periodic
Le comparatif
Compare two things. Pattern: plus / moins / aussi + adjective + que.
plus … quemore … than
moins … queless … than
aussi … queas … as
Paris est plus grande que Dublin.
Paris is bigger than Dublin.
Irregular: bon → meilleur, bien → mieux, mauvais → pire.

Superlative

Periodic
Le superlatif
"The most / the least". Pattern: le / la / les + plus / moins + adjective. Adjective agrees with the noun.
C'est la plus belle ville du monde.
It is the most beautiful city in the world.
After a superlative, use de (not dans): la plus belle ville de France.

Negation — la négation

8 forms

Q3 may ask you to put a phrase into the negative, or to find an example of a specific negation. Every French negation has two parts (ne … X) that sandwich the verb.

ne … pas

Standard
not
The basic negation. Ne goes before the verb; pas goes after. Drops to n' before a vowel.
Je ne sais pas. Il n'aime pas les épinards.
I don't know. He doesn't like spinach.
In the passé composé, both parts sandwich the auxiliary, not the participle: Je n'ai pas mangé.

ne … plus

Common
no longer / not any more
Elle ne fume plus.
She doesn't smoke any more.

ne … jamais

Common
never
Il ne m'a jamais parlé.
He has never spoken to me.

ne … rien

Periodic
nothing
Je ne vois rien.
I see nothing.
Rien can be the subject — then it comes before ne: Rien ne va !

ne … personne

Periodic
nobody / no one
Je n'ai vu personne.
I saw nobody.
In passé composé, personne goes after the past participle (unlike pas/plus/jamais/rien which go between auxiliary and participle).

ne … que

Periodic
only
"Only" — restrictive, not strictly a negation. Que goes right before the thing being restricted.
Je n'ai que dix euros.
I only have ten euros.

ne … aucun(e)

Periodic
no / not any
Aucun agrees with the noun (aucun / aucune). Always singular.
Il n'a aucune idée.
He has no idea.

ni … ni

Rare
neither … nor
Used with ne. Articles (un, une, des, du) usually drop after ni.
Il ne boit ni café ni thé.
He drinks neither coffee nor tea.
+

Adverbs, prepositions & more

5 categories

The smaller-frequency Q3 categories. Less common than tenses but still worth 5 marks each when they appear. Focus on the patterns that make them easy to spot at a glance.

Adverbs in -ment

Common
Les adverbes en -ment
Most French adverbs end in -ment (≈ English -ly). Built by adding -ment to the feminine form of the adjective.
heureux → heureuseheureusement
lent → lentelentement
vrai → vraievraiment
-ant adj.-amment (constamment)
-ent adj.-emment (récemment)
Elle parle doucement et clairement.
She speaks softly and clearly.
Common irregulars don't end in -ment: bien, mal, vite, ici, là, ainsi, déjà, encore. Still adverbs.

Prepositions

Standard
Les prépositions
Small words linking a noun to the rest of the sentence. The most common ones to spot:
àto / at
deof / from
dans · sur · sousin · on · under
avec · sanswith · without
pour · parfor · by
chezat the house of
entre · parmibetween · among
avant · aprèsbefore · after
pendant · depuisduring · since/for
Contractions: à + le = au · à + les = aux · de + le = du · de + les = des. These hide the preposition — but they still count.

Conjunctions

Periodic
Les conjonctions
Words that join clauses or words. Two main types:
Coordinatinget · ou · mais · donc · car · ni · or
Subordinatingque · parce que · quand · si · puisque
+ subjunctivebien que · pour que · avant que
Il est tard donc je rentre. Je sais que tu viens.
It's late so I'm going home. I know that you're coming.

Passive voice

Rare
La voix passive
Formed with être + past participle, often with par (by). The past participle agrees with the subject.
Find est / sont / était / sera followed by a past participle, where the action is done to the subject, not by it. Often followed by par.
La pharmacie a été cambriolée par les malfaiteurs.
The pharmacy was burgled by the criminals.
Don't confuse with the passé composé of être-verbs (elle est partie) — those are active. Passive is always être + past participle of a different verb.

Interrogatives

Periodic
Les mots interrogatifs
Question words. Q3 sometimes asks for a question in a specific form.
quiwho
que / qu'what
quoiwhat (after prep.)
oùwhere
quandwhen
commenthow
pourquoiwhy
combien (de)how much / many
Pourquoi est-elle partie ? Combien de frères as-tu ?
Why has she left? How many brothers do you have?

How Q3 is marked

Examiner notes

The cheapest marks on the paper.

Q3 is worth 30 marks (15% of Section I) for what is essentially a spot-the-word exercise. The chief examiner's report flags this section every year as the place where students systematically under-perform relative to effort required.

The marking is mechanical. There is a specific item that the examiner is looking for. If you find it and quote it cleanly, you get full marks. If you quote too much or quote the wrong form, you lose marks. There is no partial credit for "nearly right" in the same way there is for comprehension answers.

Three rules that save marks.

1. Quote only the item asked for. If asked for a verb in the conditional, quote just the verb (achèterais) — not the surrounding clause. Each extraneous word can cost a mark.

2. Quote the full form when the form has multiple words. Passé composé = a découvert (two words). Reflexive in the past = s'est levée (three words). Quoting only the participle = partial marks.

3. Copy spelling and accents exactly. Q3 answers are quotation, not your own writing. Missing accents (é vs e) or wrong spellings can lose marks even when you've identified the right word.
