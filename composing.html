01

The structure

Five steps, in order. Hit each one and you cover everything the examiner is looking for — agreement, reasoning, examples, solutions, and a memorable close.

1
Introduction — agree or disagree
State your position clearly in the very first sentence, then add 2–3 strong opening phrases that frame your answer.
Je suis tout à fait d'accord avec cette affirmation…
2
Why? What is the issue?
React to the topic — frame the debate clearly so the examiner knows you understood the prompt.
De quoi s'agit-il, en fait ? La première constatation qui s'impose, c'est que…
3
Examples — opinions, criticism, comparisons
Back up your argument with personal opinion, criticism of the status quo, and comparison to other situations.
Prenons par exemple… / C'est une situation inquiétante…
4
Solutions / outcomes / thoughts
Use conditional + subjunctive here. Propose what should happen. This is where you score top-band language marks.
Ce serait un grand pas en avant si l'on arrivait à…
5
Conclusion + idiom or proverb
Wrap up in one sentence. Then close with a relevant French proverb — examiners notice these.
Je terminerai en disant que… / Qui vivra verra.

Very likely

01

The structure

A diary entry is personal and emotional. You're writing to yourself. The examiner wants to see three tenses (passé composé, imparfait, future) and a strong emotional register.

1
Date + greeting
Always open with the date in French, then "Cher journal" or "Chère amie". Standard layout.
Cher journal, le 15 mai…
2
Set the scene
What happened? Use passé composé for completed actions and imparfait for background atmosphere. Combine them naturally.
Aujourd'hui, j'ai appris que… Il faisait beau quand…
3
Express feelings
This is what diaries score marks on. Use a wide emotional vocabulary — happiness, sadness, surprise, regret, anger, gratitude.
Je me sens tellement… Cela m'a rendu(e)…
4
Look forward + sign off
Use the future tense at least once. Sign off warmly with "À bientôt" + your name.
Demain, j'espère que… À bientôt, Marie.

01

The structure

A formal letter needs layout marks (date, address, salutation, closing). Get those right first — they're cheap marks. Then write tight, polite, formal French.

1
Layout — sender + date + recipient
Your name and address top-left, the city and date top-right, the recipient's name/role below left.
Marie Dupont, Cork, le 15 mai 2026 · Monsieur le Directeur,
2
Opening formula
Never use "Bonjour" or "Salut" in a formal letter. Use the proper salutation matching the recipient.
Monsieur, / Madame, / Madame, Monsieur,
3
State your purpose immediately
First sentence makes clear why you're writing. No small talk.
Je vous écris afin de vous informer de…
4
Closing formula
Two marks for the formal closing alone. Always include it — even if you ran out of time, write the closing.
Veuillez agréer, Monsieur, l'expression de mes sentiments distingués.
