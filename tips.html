How to use your 150 minutes

Timing is the most common reason students lose marks. Below is the examiner's recommended allocation — stick to it even if a question feels unfinished.

0:00
Read the whole paper first (5 min)
Scan Section I and II before writing a single word. Underline key terms in each question. Choose your Section II tasks.
0:05
Q1 — Text A comprehension (35 min)
Read Text A once for gist, then section by section as you answer. Answer in French throughout. Locate-and-quote = lift from text; infer = rephrase in your own words.
0:40
Q2 — Text B comprehension (35 min)
Same approach as Q1. Don't let Q1 eat into Q2 time — they're worth the same marks (60 each).
1:15
Q3 — Langue (15 min)
Grammar and vocabulary questions. 30 marks — worth about 10 minutes of focused effort. Do not over-invest here.
1:30
Section II, Part A — Short task (20 min)
50 marks for ~80–100 words. Note / message / short email. Plan in 3 minutes, write in 15, check in 2.
1:50
Section II, Part B — Extended task (50 min)
100 marks for ~150–200 words. The most valuable single question on the paper. Plan in 5 min, write in 38 min, check in 7 min. Use all three tenses. Use connectors.
2:22
Final check — verb endings & accents (8 min)
The cheapest marks on the paper. Check every past tense ending (-é, -i, -u), every agreement, every accent. These are lost marks that require zero extra knowledge to fix.
2:30
Pens down
If you've followed the guide, every section has been attempted. Resist the urge to add last-minute, unchecked sentences.
C

Comprehension tips

1
Read the questions before the text
Knowing what you're looking for makes the reading far more efficient. Underline the key focus of each question before reading paragraph one.
2
Locate-and-quote = lift exactly
For L&Q questions, copy the exact phrase from the text — do not paraphrase. Even a changed word-order loses marks.
« Citez l'expression… » = copy from text
3
Infer questions require manipulation
When the question says "correct manipulation required", the examiner expects you to rephrase the text in your own French — a direct quote scores 1 or 0.
4
Vocabulary questions: quote exactly
For "trouvez un mot qui veut dire…", find the single exact word (usually an infinitive). Extra words cause mark deductions.
"Un mot" = one word, nothing more
5
True/false: justify with a quote
Mark the statement T or F first, then support your choice with a phrase from the correct section. No quote = no full marks.
6
Section references matter
If a question directs you to "la deuxième section", your answer must come from that section only. An answer from the wrong section scores zero even if it's true.
7
Opinion question: stay text-based
The final opinion question (answered in English or Irish) requires text evidence. Opinions without textual support score the minimum content marks.
8
Two points, clearly separated
The opinion question usually asks for two points. Write them as clearly distinct paragraphs or numbered sentences. One long paragraph risks being read as one point.
W

Writing tips

1
Anchor in the stimulus
The task always gives you a stimulus — a headline, a statement, a situation. Quote or paraphrase it explicitly in your opening sentence. It earns points and focuses your answer.
2
Three tenses, minimum
The marking scheme rewards use of multiple tenses. At minimum: present (for statements) + passé composé (for what happened) + imparfait (for context/description). Throw in a future too.
Present + Passé Composé + Imparfait = base H3
3
Use three named connectors
Néanmoins, en revanche, par ailleurs — drop them naturally at the start of paragraphs. Examiners notice them. They signal a structured argument.
4
Plan before you write
Spend 3–5 minutes on a quick plan. Bullet points only. This prevents the classic mistake of a strong first paragraph and a wandering, unfinished second.
5
Formal letters need layout marks
Sender address, date, recipient name/role, salutation, body, closing formula. Two marks are awarded purely for having the layout right — free marks if you've learned the format.
6
Vary your sentence structure
Subject-verb-object gets boring fast. Try relative clauses (qui, que), gerunds (en + present participle), and infinitive constructions (pour + infinitif) to show real mastery.
En rentrant chez moi, j'ai remarqué que…
7
Don't use English sentence structure
French word order is different from English. "I love swimming" → "J'aime nager" (not "J'aime la natation" when you mean the activity). Translate thoughts, not words.
8
Close the loop in your conclusion
The final sentence should echo the opening idea. This gives the piece a circular, controlled feel that examiners reward. Use "Ainsi,", "Pour conclure,", "En fin de compte,".

Common mistakes

Grammar Forgetting past participle agreements
In the passé composé with être, the participle must agree with the subject. With avoir + preceding direct object, it must agree with the object.
→ Elle est allée (not allé) / Les livres que j'ai lus (not lu)
Grammar Mixing up passé composé & imparfait
Passé composé for completed actions; imparfait for ongoing states, descriptions, or repeated actions. Getting this wrong is the most penalised grammar error.
→ Il faisait beau (imparfait: weather context) + Je suis sorti(e) (PC: completed action)
Timing Spending too long on Section I
Many students spend 95 minutes on reading and only 55 on writing. Section II is worth the same — and writing takes longer to do well. Watch the clock.
→ Set a hard stop at 1h30 for Section I, even mid-sentence.
Layout No formal letter salutation/closing
Two layout marks are awarded for the opening salutation (Monsieur,) and closing formula. Students who skip these lose free marks.
→ Always include: Monsieur, / Madame, … Veuillez agréer…
Vocab Missing accents
Accents change meaning in French. "a" ≠ "à", "ou" ≠ "où", "e" ≠ "é". The final 8 minutes should be used to check every accent in your writing.
→ Reserve the final 8 minutes purely for accents and verb endings.
Grammar Wrong gender on nouns
Le/la, un/une, mon/ma all depend on gender. "Le problème" (not la), "la solution" (not le). Learn nouns with their article from the start.
→ Always learn: le problème, la situation, le gouvernement, la société
Timing Answering Q6 too briefly
The opinion question is 10 marks and requires two distinct, text-supported points. Many students write 20 words. Aim for 50 words across two clear points.
→ Two points, each in its own sentence, each with a text reference.
Critical Not attempting a question
A blank answer scores zero. A rough attempt in broken French can still earn 1–3 marks. Always write something — even a half-remembered phrase or educated guess.
→ Never leave a question blank. A partial answer always beats zero.
